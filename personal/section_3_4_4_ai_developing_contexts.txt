3.4.4 AI Education in Developing Contexts

While AI education has gained traction in well-resourced educational systems, its implementation in developing contexts presents distinct challenges that require tailored solutions. Ali et al. (2022), in their systematic review of global K-12 AI education, identified infrastructure dependency as the primary barrier to equitable AI literacy development. Cloud-based AI services—the dominant paradigm for educational AI tools—assume reliable high-speed internet connectivity, modern computing hardware, and continuous electrical power that are unavailable in many developing contexts including substantial portions of Ghana. The review documented that students in under-resourced schools were systematically excluded from AI education opportunities, with fewer than 5% of reviewed AI education programs successfully implemented in low-connectivity environments (Ali et al., 2022). This exclusion threatens to replicate and amplify existing digital divides, positioning AI as a technology for elite populations while excluding learners who would most benefit from understanding systems increasingly shaping economic and social opportunity.

Bhardwaj et al. (2022) addressed these infrastructure challenges through their development and evaluation of offline-first learning systems designed specifically for low-resource environments. Their research demonstrated that AI education need not require continuous connectivity: machine learning models can be pre-trained and deployed locally, inference can run on modest hardware through model optimization techniques, and curriculum content can be distributed through offline media for contexts where internet access is limited or expensive. The study established proof-of-concept for lightweight AI implementations—including image classification, sentiment analysis, and simple neural network demonstrations—that function on low-cost hardware without cloud dependency (Bhardwaj et al., 2022). These findings suggest that AI education in developing contexts requires technological architectures fundamentally different from those optimized for resource-rich environments, prioritizing offline capability, low bandwidth requirements, and local processing over cloud-dependent services.

Mobile-first approaches offer particular promise for AI education in contexts with limited traditional computing infrastructure but high smartphone penetration. Oyelere et al. (2017) documented that mobile devices have achieved near-ubiquitous penetration in Sub-Saharan African contexts, with smartphones often representing households' primary computing resource. However, the researchers noted that mobile AI education presents distinct design challenges: smaller screens constrain complex visualizations, touch interfaces differ fundamentally from mouse-keyboard interactions assumed by most educational software, and battery limitations affect usage patterns. Successful mobile AI education, according to their analysis, requires interface designs specifically optimized for mobile contexts rather than merely porting desktop applications to smaller screens.

The curriculum content for AI education in developing contexts requires cultural adaptation beyond mere translation. Ali et al. (2022) emphasized that AI case studies and examples drawn from Western contexts—recommendation algorithms for streaming services, voice assistants for smart homes, autonomous vehicles—may have limited resonance for learners in agrarian economies or informal urban settlements. Effective AI education in Ghanaian contexts requires locally relevant examples: AI applications in cocoa farming and agricultural extension, machine learning for malaria diagnosis and tropical disease prediction, natural language processing for Ghanaian languages and dialects. These contextualized examples serve dual purposes: they make AI concepts concretely understandable while simultaneously demonstrating AI's relevance to learners' communities and potential future careers.

Teacher preparation for AI education presents amplified challenges in developing contexts. Adarkwah et al. (2022) documented that Ghanaian educators face severe capacity constraints for technology integration generally, with AI-specific expertise virtually absent from the teaching workforce. The researchers emphasized that sustainable AI education in Ghana cannot depend on importing AI-literate teachers but must develop local capacity through systematic professional development, communities of practice, and scaffolded curriculum materials that support non-expert educators. This reality informed Kone Academy's design of extensive automated guidance systems and teacher support resources that enable effective AI instruction without requiring teachers to possess deep AI technical knowledge.

Assessment of AI literacy in developing contexts requires approaches that account for infrastructure limitations. Traditional computer-based assessments assume reliable devices and connectivity; paper-based assessments cannot evaluate practical AI skills. Shute et al. (2023) recommended portfolio-based and project-based assessment approaches that capture learner AI literacy through artifact creation, documentation, and demonstrated application—methods compatible with intermittent connectivity and varied hardware availability. These assessment approaches also align with constructionist pedagogical principles, evaluating learning through tangible creation rather than standardized testing.

For the Kone Academy platform, research on AI education in developing contexts has directly shaped Level 3 (Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning) architectural and pedagogical decisions. The platform employs offline-first design with lightweight, locally-running AI models that demonstrate machine learning concepts without cloud dependency. Mobile-optimized interfaces ensure accessibility on the devices most Ghanaian learners actually possess. Culturally-relevant case studies drawn from African applications ground AI concepts in contexts meaningful to learners. Extensive scaffolding and automated guidance support teachers without AI expertise. Portfolio-based assessment captures learning outcomes without requiring continuous connectivity or specialized testing infrastructure. By addressing the specific challenges documented in developing context AI education research, Kone Academy seeks to extend AI literacy access to learners systematically excluded by infrastructure-dependent approaches—positioning African youth not as passive consumers of AI technologies developed elsewhere but as informed critics and eventual creators of AI systems appropriate for African contexts and needs.
