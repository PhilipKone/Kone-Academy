3.8.2 Systematic Reviews on Gender in Computer Science

The gender gap in computer science has been extensively examined through systematic reviews that synthesize empirical evidence on causes, manifestations, and intervention effectiveness. Grissom et al. (2021) conducted a comprehensive systematic review examining gender disparities in computing education, analyzing 61 empirical studies published between 2014 and 2019. Their analysis confirmed persistent underrepresentation of girls and women across computing education contexts, while identifying that disparities are not uniform—they vary across educational levels, cultural contexts, and computing subdisciplines. The review documented that gender gaps are smallest in early elementary computing activities, widen substantially during adolescence, and reach their maximum in higher education and professional computing—suggesting that educational interventions may have their greatest impact when implemented before secondary school when gaps are still relatively narrow (Grissom et al., 2021).

Margolis et al. (2023) contributed to this literature through a systematic review specifically examining intervention strategies for addressing gender disparities in K-12 computing. Their analysis of 47 intervention studies identified several approaches with demonstrated effectiveness: mentorship programs connecting girls with female computing professionals, extracurricular computing clubs providing stereotype-free learning environments, curriculum modifications that emphasize social relevance and creative applications of computing, and explicit instruction addressing stereotype threat and growth mindset. The review emphasized that single-intervention approaches show limited sustained impact, while comprehensive programs combining multiple strategies produce more durable changes in participation and persistence (Margolis et al., 2023).

The mechanisms through which effective interventions operate extend beyond mere exposure to computing activities. Grissom et al. (2021) documented that successful interventions address both individual-level factors (self-efficacy, interest, identity) and environmental factors (stereotypes, peer culture, classroom climate). Interventions focusing solely on building girls' technical skills without addressing social context show limited impact, as girls may develop capability without developing the sense of belonging that sustains participation. Conversely, interventions that create supportive peer communities and counter stereotypical messaging enable girls to maintain engagement even when encountering technical challenges that might otherwise prompt disengagement.

The cultural specificity of gender and computing represents an important consideration for intervention design. While systematic reviews by Grissom et al. (2021) and Margolis et al. (2023) were conducted primarily with Western contexts, the fundamental mechanisms—stereotype threat, self-efficacy, sense of belonging—appear transferable across cultures, even as their specific manifestations may differ. For Ghanaian contexts, where traditional gender roles may interact with computing stereotypes in distinctive ways, interventions must attend to local cultural norms while addressing universal barriers to girls' participation.

For Kone Academy, research on gender in computer science informs platform design decisions intended to support equitable participation. The platform incorporates diverse role models and exemplars, emphasizes collaborative and creative computing activities, provides multiple pathways for success that accommodate different learning approaches, and includes explicit attention to growth mindset and stereotype inoculation. By implementing evidence-based strategies from systematic reviews while attending to Ghanaian cultural contexts, Kone Academy seeks to contribute to gender equity in computing education.
