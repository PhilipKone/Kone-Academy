3.9 Research Gaps and Opportunities

The comprehensive literature review presented in preceding sections reveals substantial research attention to coding education, robotics integration, AI literacy, and educational technology in well-resourced Western contexts. However, significant gaps persist that limit the applicability of existing knowledge to developing contexts such as Ghana and constrain the evidence base for designing effective educational technology interventions. This section identifies critical research gaps that Kone Academy's development and evaluation seek to address, positioning the platform as both a practical intervention and a research contribution.

**Limited African Context Research**

Despite growing recognition of digital equity imperatives, educational technology research remains heavily concentrated in North American and European contexts. Ali et al. (2022), in their systematic review of AI education, documented that fewer than 5% of published studies examined implementations in African or other developing contexts. Similarly, research on block-based programming efficacy, robotics education, and computational thinking development derives predominantly from studies conducted in well-resourced schools with reliable infrastructure, experienced teachers, and abundant technical support. The extent to which findings from these contexts transfer to Ghanaian schools—with intermittent connectivity, limited computing hardware, and teachers without computer science backgrounds—remains empirically unexamined. This contextual research gap undermines confidence in applying existing design principles to Kone Academy's Ghanaian implementation and necessitates context-specific research to validate or adapt established approaches.

**The Block-to-Text Transition Gap**

A persistent challenge in computing education research involves the transition from block-based to text-based programming. While research has documented the difficulty of this transition (Weintrop & Wilensky, 2017), limited evidence addresses how to scaffold it effectively, particularly for learners who may never progress to professional programming but would benefit from computational thinking development. The research literature presents a dichotomy—either block-based introductory tools or professional text-based environments—with insufficient attention to intermediate scaffolding that might enable smoother progression. Kone Academy's robotics level (Level 2) explicitly targets this gap by maintaining visual programming while introducing physical computing complexity, but empirical research on whether this approach effectively bridges the block-text divide remains limited. This "middle space" between introductory blocks and professional text represents a significant research opportunity.

**Integrated Coding→Robotics→AI Progression Studies**

Existing research typically examines coding education, robotics, and AI as separate domains, with limited investigation of integrated curricula that progressively develop competencies across all three areas. Long and Magerko (2020) noted that AI literacy frameworks remain disconnected from established computational thinking and programming curricula, while robotics education research often treats physical computing as an end in itself rather than as scaffolding for more abstract concepts. The pedagogical and technical challenges of designing coherent three-tiered progressions—from virtual coding through physical robotics to abstract AI—have received insufficient research attention. Kone Academy's integrated curriculum design addresses this gap practically, but longitudinal research examining whether such integration produces synergistic learning benefits beyond separate domain instruction remains needed.

**Offline-First Platform Pedagogical Research**

The technical architectures enabling offline-first educational technology have advanced substantially (Bhardwaj et al., 2022), yet pedagogical research on how offline functionality shapes learning experiences, collaborative activities, and assessment validity remains limited. Most educational technology research assumes continuous connectivity, examining online collaboration, cloud-based resources, and real-time feedback mechanisms. The extent to which pedagogical strategies require adaptation for offline-capable environments—where synchronous collaboration is constrained, resources must be pre-loaded, and assessments queue for later transmission—represents an unexamined domain. Kone Academy's offline-first design necessitates pedagogical approaches distinct from connectivity-dependent platforms, but research evidence guiding such adaptation is scarce.

**Long-Term Outcome Studies**

Perhaps the most significant gap in coding education research concerns long-term outcomes. The majority of published studies examine immediate learning gains over periods of weeks or months, with few tracking participants beyond initial exposure to assess sustained interest, continued participation, career choices, or application of computational thinking to novel problems. For developing contexts where educational investments must demonstrate value against competing resource needs, evidence of long-term impact is particularly crucial yet largely absent. Kone Academy's evaluation framework prioritizes longitudinal data collection to address this gap, but the field as a whole requires substantially more long-term outcome research to justify continued investment in coding education initiatives.

**Conclusion**

These research gaps collectively represent opportunities for Kone Academy to contribute both practical innovation and research knowledge. By implementing and evaluating a platform specifically designed for Ghanaian infrastructure constraints, with explicit attention to block-text transition scaffolding, integrated curriculum progression, offline-first pedagogical adaptation, and long-term outcome tracking, Kone Academy addresses documented limitations in the existing research base. The following section details platform design decisions grounded in this gap analysis, describing how Kone Academy translates literature-informed principles into contextually-adapted implementation.
