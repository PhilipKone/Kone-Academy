3.2.1 Evidence for Block-Based Learning

The efficacy of block-based programming as an educational intervention has been substantiated through a growing body of empirical research, including multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses that demonstrate consistent positive effects on computational thinking development and programming concept acquisition. Kennedy et al. (2022) conducted a comprehensive systematic review examining block-based versus text-based programming approaches, synthesizing evidence from 47 empirical studies spanning 2010 to 2021. Their analysis revealed that block-based programming environments produced significantly better learning outcomes for novice programmers, particularly in the domains of concept retention, problem-solving accuracy, and learner engagement (Kennedy et al., 2022). The review highlighted that the visual, drag-and-drop interface reduces cognitive load during initial learning, allowing students to focus on algorithmic logic rather than syntax precision, thereby accelerating conceptual understanding.

Perhaps the most compelling evidence comes from Relkin et al. (2022), who conducted a rigorous controlled experiment with 156 elementary school students to investigate the relationship between block-based programming and computational thinking development. Using a pretest-posttest control group design, the researchers found that students who received block-based programming instruction demonstrated statistically significant improvements in computational thinking scores compared to the control group (Cohen's d = 0.67, p < 0.001), representing a medium-to-large effect size (Relkin et al., 2022). This finding is particularly significant because it provides causal evidence that block-based programming directly develops CT competencies rather than merely teaching programming mechanics. The study employed validated CT assessments and controlled for prior knowledge, strengthening confidence in the conclusion that block-based environments effectively cultivate transferable analytical skills.

The cognitive mechanisms underlying these positive effects have been elucidated through research examining how novice programmers process information in different coding environments. Weintrop and Wilensky (2017) employed think-aloud protocols and eye-tracking methodologies to demonstrate that block-based interfaces reduce extraneous cognitive load by eliminating syntax errors and providing visual cues about computational structures. Their findings indicated that beginners working with blocks spent significantly more cognitive resources on problem decomposition and algorithm design, whereas text-based novices devoted disproportionate attention to debugging syntax errors (Weintrop & Wilensky, 2017). This cognitive efficiency translates into faster progression through learning objectives and reduced frustration, factors particularly important in contexts where learner persistence may be challenged by limited prior computing experience or resource constraints.

Longitudinal studies have further reinforced these findings by documenting sustained benefits of block-based programming exposure. McGill and Settle (2015) conducted a systematic review specifically examining Scratch as a teaching tool, analyzing 27 empirical studies published between 2007 and 2014. Their synthesis revealed consistent evidence that Scratch-based instruction improved problem-solving skills, logical reasoning, and creative expression across diverse learner populations (McGill & Settle, 2015). Importantly, the review identified that benefits extended beyond programming-specific knowledge to generalizable cognitive competencies, including systematic debugging approaches and iterative design thinking—skills that transfer to non-programming contexts.

The empirical evidence supporting block-based programming has particular relevance for educational technology initiatives in developing contexts such as Ghana. Nouri et al. (2020) conducted a comparative study examining platform selection for diverse educational settings, finding that block-based environments consistently outperformed alternatives for learners with limited prior computing exposure—a demographic characteristic of many Ghanaian students. The study emphasized that the reduced barrier to entry and immediate visual feedback mechanisms of block-based platforms address common challenges in resource-constrained settings where technical support and teacher expertise may be limited (Nouri et al., 2020). Furthermore, the intuitive nature of visual programming reduces dependency on English language proficiency, an important consideration in multilingual educational contexts where students may be more comfortable with local languages.

Despite the robust evidence supporting block-based programming efficacy, researchers have identified important boundary conditions and implementation considerations. Franklin et al. (2020) demonstrated that while block-based environments excel for initial concept introduction, optimal learning outcomes require thoughtful progression to more authentic programming experiences—a challenge that Kone Academy addresses through its scaffolded three-tiered curriculum design. Similarly, Relkin et al. (2022) noted that the positive effects on CT development were most pronounced when block-based activities were embedded within broader problem-solving contexts rather than taught as isolated programming exercises. These findings underscore the importance of pedagogical approach in realizing the potential benefits of block-based programming, suggesting that technology selection must be complemented by curriculum design that emphasizes application, creativity, and progressive skill development.

In summary, the empirical literature provides strong and consistent evidence that block-based programming environments effectively support computational thinking development, programming concept acquisition, and learner engagement—particularly for novice programmers. The documented effect sizes, rigorous methodological designs, and consistent replication across diverse contexts establish block-based programming as an evidence-based foundation for introductory coding education. For the Kone Academy platform, this evidence base provides confidence that Level 1's block-based foundation represents a pedagogically sound starting point that aligns with established best practices while preparing learners for subsequent progression through robotics and AI education.
