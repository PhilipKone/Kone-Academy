3.4.1 AI Literacy Frameworks

The integration of artificial intelligence education into K-12 curricula represents a rapidly evolving field that has gained substantial momentum following the widespread adoption of AI technologies across society. Touretzky et al. (2019) provided foundational guidance for this integration through their influential paper "Envisioning AI for K-12: What Should Every Child Know about AI?" which proposed the "Five Big Ideas in AI" framework. This framework identifies five conceptual pillars that constitute essential AI literacy: (1) Perception—computers perceive the world using sensors; (2) Representation and Reasoning—agents maintain representations of the world and use them for reasoning; (3) Learning—computers can learn from data; (4) Natural Interaction—intelligent agents require diverse knowledge to interact naturally with humans; and (5) Societal Impact—AI can impact society in both positive and negative ways (Touretzky et al., 2019). These five concepts provide a structured approach to introducing AI concepts across grade levels while avoiding the technical complexity that would overwhelm K-12 learners.

The AI4K12 Initiative, established by the Association for the Advancement of Artificial Intelligence (AAAI) and the Computer Science Teachers Association (CSTA), has operationalized Touretzky's framework through the development of detailed guidelines and learning progressions for AI education. Long and Magerko (2020) described this initiative as the first systematic attempt to define what constitutes AI literacy for primary and secondary education, emphasizing that AI education should focus on conceptual understanding rather than technical implementation. The AI4K12 guidelines organize AI concepts into grade bands (K-2, 3-5, 6-8, 9-12) with increasing complexity, ensuring age-appropriate progression from concrete experiences with AI technologies to abstract understanding of machine learning algorithms and neural networks (Long & Magerko, 2020).

Research on AI literacy development has identified critical competencies that extend beyond technical knowledge to include ethical reasoning and critical evaluation of AI systems. Ali et al. (2022) conducted a comprehensive survey of AI education in K-12 contexts across 20 countries, finding that effective AI literacy programs must address not only how AI systems function but also how they can fail, how they reflect human biases, and how they impact employment, privacy, and social structures. The study emphasized that AI education should develop "AI savvy"—the ability to interact critically and effectively with AI systems rather than simply understanding their technical mechanisms (Ali et al., 2022). This broader conceptualization of AI literacy aligns with Wing's (2006) framing of computational thinking as a fundamental literacy applicable across domains.

Chiu et al. (2021) contributed to this emerging field through their systematic review of AI education in K-12, identifying pedagogical approaches that effectively develop AI understanding among young learners. The review highlighted that successful AI education programs employ hands-on activities with pre-built AI tools (such as image classification or sentiment analysis applications) before introducing underlying algorithms, enabling learners to experience AI capabilities concretely before attempting to understand abstract mechanisms (Chiu et al., 2021). This experiential approach mirrors constructionist principles and provides a pedagogical foundation for introducing AI concepts without requiring advanced mathematics or programming prerequisites.

The competency progression for AI literacy presents unique challenges compared to traditional programming education. While programming concepts follow relatively linear progression from sequences to conditionals to loops to functions, AI concepts require understanding probabilistic reasoning, training data dependencies, and emergent behaviors that may contradict deterministic programming intuitions. Touretzky et al. (2019) addressed this challenge by recommending that initial AI exposure focus on perception and societal impact—concepts that can be understood through observation and discussion—before progressing to learning and reasoning mechanisms that require more technical background. This progression aligns with established learning science principles of moving from concrete to abstract understanding.

For the Ghanaian educational context, AI literacy frameworks must be adapted to address infrastructure limitations and curriculum integration challenges. Ali et al. (2022) noted that AI education in developing contexts faces particular obstacles including limited internet connectivity (required for many cloud-based AI services), scarce computing hardware capable of running AI workloads, and teacher preparation gaps. However, the researchers also identified opportunities: offline-capable AI tools, mobile-first approaches leveraging smartphone penetration, and the cultural relevance of AI applications in agriculture, healthcare, and language translation for multilingual African contexts (Ali et al., 2022). These findings suggest that effective AI education in Ghana requires not merely importing frameworks developed for resource-rich contexts but adapting them to local technological and cultural realities.

For the Kone Academy platform, research on AI literacy frameworks directly informs the design of Level 3 (Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning). The Five Big Ideas framework provides a structure for organizing AI content, while the AI4K12 grade band guidelines inform the targeting of concepts to appropriate developmental levels. Kone Academy's design prioritizes the "Perception" and "Learning" big ideas through hands-on activities with image classification and pattern recognition—capabilities that can be implemented on modest hardware—while addressing "Societal Impact" through locally relevant case studies examining AI applications in African agriculture, healthcare, and education. By grounding Level 3 in established AI literacy frameworks while adapting implementation for offline functionality and local context, the platform extends AI education access to learners who would otherwise be excluded by resource constraints or curriculum gaps.
