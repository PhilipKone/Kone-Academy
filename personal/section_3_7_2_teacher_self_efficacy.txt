3.7.2 Teacher Self-Efficacy in Computer Science

Teacher self-efficacy—the belief in one's capability to organize and execute courses of action required to produce given attainments—represents a critical but often overlooked factor in computer science education implementation. Bean et al. (2015) conducted seminal research examining elementary teachers' self-efficacy beliefs regarding computer science instruction, finding that many teachers, despite positive attitudes toward technology integration, harbor significant doubts about their ability to effectively teach coding and computational thinking concepts. Their study documented that teachers with lower self-efficacy in computer science were less likely to implement CS activities, more likely to abandon lessons when encountering technical difficulties, and less effective at supporting student problem-solving—demonstrating that confidence gaps directly translate into educational opportunity gaps for students (Bean et al., 2015).

The sources of low self-efficacy in computer science teaching are multifaceted and differ from self-efficacy concerns in traditional subject areas. Bean et al. (2015) identified that many teachers perceive computer science as fundamentally different from their areas of expertise—requiring logical reasoning and technical troubleshooting skills they doubt they possess. Unlike reading or mathematics, where teachers typically have extensive personal experience as successful learners, many teachers have never learned to code and thus lack the experiential foundation that supports teaching confidence. This expertise gap is particularly acute in developing contexts such as Ghana, where teacher preparation programs have historically not included computer science coursework, leaving practicing teachers without any formal foundation in computational thinking or programming (Adarkwah et al., 2022).

The consequences of low teacher self-efficacy extend beyond reduced implementation to affect the quality of instruction when CS activities do occur. Bean et al. (2015) documented that teachers with low CS self-efficacy tend to rely heavily on scripted activities, avoid open-ended exploration that might expose their knowledge limitations, and deflect student questions they cannot answer rather than modeling productive struggle. These pedagogical adaptations, while protective of teacher confidence, severely constrain student learning opportunities—limiting the development of problem-solving skills, creative expression, and conceptual understanding that effective CS education should cultivate.

Building teacher self-efficacy in computer science requires approaches that differ from traditional professional development models. Bean et al. (2015) found that self-efficacy improved most significantly when teachers experienced personal success with coding activities similar to those they would facilitate for students—not when they received abstract training on pedagogical strategies. This finding suggests that teacher preparation for platforms such as Kone Academy should prioritize hands-on experience with the actual curriculum, enabling teachers to complete projects, debug programs, and experience the constructionist learning cycle before attempting classroom facilitation. The researchers emphasized that early success experiences are particularly crucial; initial failures with coding can reinforce self-efficacy doubts that are difficult to overcome.

For the Ghanaian educational context, teacher self-efficacy challenges are compounded by additional factors including limited technical support infrastructure, scarce peer expertise for consultation, and cultural beliefs that position technology as a masculine domain potentially inappropriate for female teachers. Adarkwah et al. (2022) documented that Ghanaian teachers often lack confidence not merely in their technical skills but in their legitimacy as technology educators—doubting whether they possess the authority to teach subjects they perceive as requiring specialized expertise. Kone Academy's design addresses these confidence barriers through extensive automated scaffolding that reduces teacher technical burden, comprehensive teacher guides that anticipate common questions, and community features that enable peer support across geographical distances.

The relationship between teacher self-efficacy and student outcomes in computer science education remains an active research area with significant implications for platform design. Bean et al. (2015) established that teacher confidence influences both whether CS education occurs and how effectively it is implemented when it does occur. For Kone Academy, this research supports investment in teacher support infrastructure that extends beyond initial training to ongoing confidence-building through scaffolded professional development, success-oriented onboarding sequences, and community features that enable teachers to share victories and troubleshoot challenges collectively. By addressing teacher self-efficacy as a fundamental implementation prerequisite rather than an afterthought, Kone Academy seeks to ensure that platform capabilities translate into classroom realities—recognizing that even the most sophisticated educational technology cannot improve student learning without confident, capable teachers to facilitate its use.
