3.4.2 AI Ethics and Responsible AI

The rapid deployment of artificial intelligence technologies across societal domains has intensified focus on ethical considerations, particularly regarding algorithmic bias, fairness, transparency, and accountability. Perrotti and Howard (2022) conducted a systematic review examining AI ethics education in K-12 contexts, synthesizing findings from 34 empirical studies published between 2016 and 2021. Their analysis revealed that while technical AI literacy has received substantial attention, ethics education remains underdeveloped in most K-12 AI curricula, with fewer than 30% of reviewed programs incorporating structured ethical reasoning components (Perrotti & Howard, 2022). This gap is particularly concerning given that children and adolescents are increasingly interacting with AI systems—recommendation algorithms, voice assistants, automated assessment tools—without critical frameworks for evaluating these systems' limitations, biases, or societal impacts.

The systematic review identified several recurring ethical themes that K-12 AI education should address. Algorithmic bias emerged as the most frequently discussed concern, with studies documenting that children as young as 10 years old can understand and identify biased outcomes when presented with concrete examples (Perrotti & Howard, 2022). Fairness in AI systems—encompassing both distributive fairness (who receives benefits and harms) and procedural fairness (transparency and due process)—represents a conceptually accessible entry point for introducing AI ethics to younger learners. The review emphasized that effective ethics education employs real-world case studies rather than abstract principles, enabling learners to connect ethical concepts to their lived experiences with technology (Perrotti & Howard, 2022).

Privacy and data rights constitute another critical dimension of AI ethics education. Perrotti and Howard (2022) documented that K-12 learners generally lack understanding of how AI systems collect, process, and monetize personal data. Effective educational interventions, according to the review, employ experiential approaches where learners examine the data trails they generate through digital activities and consider what inferences AI systems might draw from this information. These activities develop "data awareness"—an understanding of personal data as a resource with value and risks—without requiring technical knowledge of database systems or machine learning architectures.

Age-appropriate ethics pedagogy presents distinct challenges compared to technical AI education. While young learners can engage with concrete examples of biased algorithms or privacy violations, abstract ethical frameworks (utilitarianism, deontological ethics, virtue ethics) exceed typical K-12 cognitive development. Perrotti and Howard (2022) recommended "ethical reasoning skills" over "ethical theory instruction" for K-12 contexts: teaching learners to identify stakeholders, consider multiple perspectives, evaluate consequences, and articulate value-based positions rather than applying formal ethical frameworks. This skills-based approach aligns with established moral education research and enables progressive development of ethical reasoning from concrete examples to abstract principles.

The cultural dimension of AI ethics education has particular relevance for non-Western contexts including Ghana. Perrotti and Howard (2022) noted that most AI ethics education materials are developed in Western contexts and may not resonate with learners from diverse cultural backgrounds. Concepts such as individual privacy rights, algorithmic fairness, and AI accountability reflect Western liberal democratic values that may not align with collectivist cultural frameworks prevalent in many African societies. The review recommended adapting AI ethics case studies to local contexts—examining AI applications in agriculture, healthcare, or governance that are culturally relevant to learners' communities—while maintaining universal ethical principles regarding human dignity and non-discrimination.

For the Ghanaian educational context, AI ethics education must address specific vulnerabilities and opportunities. Bias in AI systems trained predominantly on Western data sets can produce discriminatory outcomes for African populations—facial recognition systems with higher error rates for Black faces, natural language processing that fails with African English variants or local languages, healthcare algorithms that underrepresent tropical diseases. Adarkwah et al. (2022) emphasized that ethics education in developing contexts should empower learners to identify and challenge such biases, positioning African youth not merely as consumers of AI technologies developed elsewhere but as critical evaluators and eventually creators of AI systems appropriate for African contexts.

For the Kone Academy platform, research on AI ethics informs the design of Level 3's "Societal Impact" component within the Five Big Ideas framework. The platform incorporates age-appropriate ethics modules that employ Ghanaian case studies—examining AI applications in cocoa farming, malaria diagnosis, or local language translation—to develop ethical reasoning skills grounded in learners' cultural context. These modules emphasize practical skills (identifying bias, evaluating fairness, protecting privacy) over abstract theory, aligning with Perrotti and Howard's (2022) recommendations for K-12 ethics pedagogy. By integrating ethics throughout Level 3 rather than treating it as an isolated module, Kone Academy seeks to develop what Long and Magerko (2020) termed "responsible AI savvy"—the ability to use AI technologies critically, ethically, and effectively.
