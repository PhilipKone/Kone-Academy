3.2.4 Comparative Studies

While individual studies have documented the efficacy of specific block-based platforms, comparative research examining relative affordances across environments provides important guidance for educational technology selection. Nouri et al. (2020) conducted a comprehensive comparative study analyzing programming platforms for K-12 education, evaluating Scratch, Blockly, Alice, and Greenfoot across multiple pedagogical and technical dimensions. Their analysis revealed significant differences in platform suitability depending on learner characteristics, learning objectives, and contextual constraints—demonstrating that "block-based" is not a monolithic category but encompasses diverse implementations with distinct strengths and limitations (Nouri et al., 2020).

Scratch emerged from this comparison as particularly effective for fostering creativity and collaborative learning, aligning with its design philosophy of "low floors, wide walls, high ceilings." The platform's integrated online community, extensive media library, and emphasis on creative self-expression through interactive stories and games make it highly engaging for younger learners (ages 8-12). However, Nouri et al. (2020) identified limitations in Scratch's extensibility for advanced computing concepts and its dependency on internet connectivity for community features—constraints that may limit applicability in developing contexts with intermittent connectivity. The researchers noted that while Scratch excels at initial engagement, its closed ecosystem can create challenges for learners seeking to transition toward more authentic programming experiences or apply their skills outside the Scratch environment.

Blockly-based platforms, conversely, demonstrated greater flexibility and customizability in comparative analysis. The framework's modular architecture enables educators to create tailored programming environments aligned with specific curriculum requirements, programming languages, or contextual needs. Nouri et al. (2020) documented that Blockly implementations showed particular strengths in structured educational settings where learning objectives are clearly defined and progression pathways are intentionally designed. The framework's code generation capabilities—producing executable code in multiple text-based languages—provide explicit bridges toward authentic programming, addressing transition challenges that Scratch's closed ecosystem cannot. However, the comparative study noted that Blockly platforms typically require more substantial development resources and technical expertise to implement effectively, potentially limiting accessibility for educators without programming backgrounds.

The comparative analysis also examined platform suitability across diverse learner populations. Nouri et al. (2020) found that platform selection significantly influenced outcomes for learners with varying prior computing experience, language backgrounds, and accessibility needs. For novice programmers with limited English proficiency, the visual nature of block-based environments provided equitable access regardless of language background—a finding with particular relevance for multilingual contexts such as Ghana where English may be a second or third language for many learners. However, the researchers emphasized that platform selection alone is insufficient; effective implementation requires pedagogical approaches that leverage platform affordances while addressing contextual challenges.

For Kone Academy, this comparative research supports the platform's design decision to build upon Blockly's customizable architecture rather than adapting Scratch's closed ecosystem. While Scratch's community features and creative emphasis are valuable, the platform's requirements for offline functionality, local curriculum alignment, and scaffolded progression toward text-based programming align more closely with Blockly's technical affordances. By leveraging Blockly's customization capabilities while designing pedagogical experiences that incorporate Scratch's constructionist engagement principles, Kone Academy seeks to combine the strengths identified across comparative research while addressing the specific constraints of the Ghanaian educational context—particularly infrastructure limitations and curriculum integration requirements (Nouri et al., 2020; Milošević, 2021).
