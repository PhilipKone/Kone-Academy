Appendix C: Assessment Instruments

C.1 Computational Thinking Assessment Instrument

C.1.1 Pre-Test and Post-Test Versions

Instrument Overview:
- 45-item multiple-choice assessment
- 5 competency subscales (9 items each)
- Adapted from validated STEM CT Assessment
- Ghanaian context integration throughout
- Reading level appropriate for JHS 1-3 students

Administration Protocol:
- 45-minute time limit
- Individual administration in quiet environment
- Paper and pencil format with optional digital administration
- Standardized instructions and practice items
- Accommodations available for students with special needs

Scoring System:
- 1 point per correct item (45 points maximum)
- Subscale scores (0-9 points each)
- Conversion to scaled scores for statistical analysis
- Reliability coefficients: α = .89 (overall), .82-.87 (subscales)
- Validity established through expert review and pilot testing

C.1.2 Scoring Rubrics and Criteria

Algorithmic Thinking Subscale:
Items assess ability to:
- Create step-by-step problem-solving procedures
- Identify sequential relationships in problem contexts
- Evaluate efficiency of different solution approaches
- Apply algorithmic thinking to real-world scenarios

Sample Item Type:
"Kwame wants to program a robot to water plants in a garden. Which sequence of steps would be most efficient?"
A) Water all plants randomly
B) Water plants in rows from left to right
C) Water plants starting from the center
D) Water plants based on their height

Pattern Recognition Subscale:
Items assess ability to:
- Identify repeating patterns in data
- Extend patterns based on identified rules
- Apply pattern recognition to problem-solving
- Analyze patterns in cultural contexts

Sample Item Type:
"Adinkra symbols often follow specific patterns. If the pattern is Circle-Star-Triangle-Circle-Star-____, what comes next?"
A) Circle
B) Star
C) Triangle
D) Square

Decomposition Subscale:
Items assess ability to:
- Break complex problems into smaller components
- Identify essential problem elements
- Organize problem components logically
- Apply decomposition to Ghanaian contexts

Sample Item Type:
"A school wants to organize a cultural festival. Which is the best way to break this task into smaller parts?"
A) Do everything at once
B) Plan food, then music, then decorations
C) Only plan the food
D) Ask parents to do everything

Abstraction Subscale:
Items assess ability to:
- Focus on essential information
- Ignore irrelevant details
- Create general representations
- Apply abstraction to problem-solving

Sample Item Type:
"When programming a game about market trading, which details are most important to include?"
A) All possible market items
B) Essential trading rules and prices
C) Market weather conditions
D) Trader clothing descriptions

Debugging Subscale:
Items assess ability to:
- Identify errors in code sequences
- Systematically test solutions
- Apply debugging strategies
- Learn from error correction

Sample Item Type:
"A program to calculate market costs is giving wrong answers. What should you check first?"
A) The computer's color settings
B) The calculation formulas
C) The keyboard layout
D) The time of day

C.1.3 Validity and Reliability Documentation

Content Validity:
- Expert review by 5 Ghanaian CS education specialists
- Item relevance ratings: M = 4.6/5.0
- Cultural appropriateness confirmed through pilot testing
- Alignment with Ghanaian curriculum standards verified

Construct Validity:
- Confirmatory factor analysis supports 5-factor structure
- Factor loadings: .62-.84
- Goodness-of-fit indices: CFI = .94, RMSEA = .04
- Convergent validity established with related measures

Reliability Analysis:
- Internal consistency: α = .89 (overall)
- Subscale reliability: α = .82-.87
- Test-retest reliability: r = .81 (2-week interval)
- Inter-rater reliability: κ = .87 (scoring agreement)

C.1.4 Item Analysis and Difficulty Levels

Difficulty Index (P-values):
- Easy items (P > .70): 15 items (33%)
- Medium items (P = .30-.70): 22 items (49%)
- Difficult items (P < .30): 8 items (18%)

Discrimination Index:
- Excellent discrimination (D > .40): 28 items (62%)
- Good discrimination (D = .30-.40): 12 items (27%)
- Poor discrimination (D < .30): 5 items (11%) - flagged for revision

Item Response Theory Analysis:
- 3-parameter logistic model applied
- Item parameters: a (discrimination), b (difficulty), c (guessing)
- Test information function peaks at θ = 0.0 (average ability)
- Standard error of measurement < .30 across ability range

C.1.5 Adaptation Process for Ghanaian Context

Cultural Adaptation Procedures:
- Translation review by bilingual education specialists
- Context replacement with Ghanaian examples
- Cultural relevance validation through focus groups
- Pilot testing with 120 students across 3 regions

Linguistic Adaptation:
- Vocabulary simplification for JHS reading level
- Local terminology integration where appropriate
- Bilingual administration options (English/Twi)
- Reading time optimization for local students

Contextual Examples:
- Market trading scenarios
- Agricultural problem contexts
- Cultural celebration planning
- Community service applications
- School activity organization

C.2 Student Attitude Survey

Scale Construction:
- 12-item Likert scale (1 = Strongly Disagree, 5 = Strongly Agree)
- Developed based on established interest measures
- Ghanaian context adaptation
- Reading level appropriate for target population

Sample Items:
1. "I enjoy learning about computers and programming"
2. "Computing activities are interesting to me"
3. "I want to learn more about how computers work"
4. "Computer science is one of my favorite subjects"

Psychometric Properties:
- Internal consistency: α = .87

- Factor analysis supports single-factor structure
- Validated against behavioral engagement measures

C.4 Interview Protocols

Professional Development Experience Questions:
1. "Can you describe your experience with the Kone Academy professional development?"
2. "What aspects of the training were most helpful for your teaching?"
3. "What challenges did you face during the professional development?"
4. "How well did the PD prepare you for actual classroom implementation?"

Implementation Challenges Exploration:
5. "What difficulties have you encountered while implementing Kone Academy?"
6. "How have you addressed these challenges?"
7. "What support would have made implementation easier?"
8. "How did infrastructure limitations affect your teaching?"

Pedagogical Adaptation Strategies:
9. "How have you adapted the platform activities for your students?"
10. "What teaching strategies have worked best with this technology?"
11. "How do you manage students with different skill levels?"
12. "How do you integrate computing activities with other subjects?"

Support Needs Assessment:
13. "What ongoing support do you need for successful implementation?"
14. "How can the platform be improved to better serve your needs?"
15. "What resources would help you teach computing more effectively?"

Sustainability Planning Discussion:
16. "Do you plan to continue using Kone Academy after the research ends?"
17. "What would enable you to sustain this implementation?"
18. "What advice would you give to teachers just starting with this platform?"
