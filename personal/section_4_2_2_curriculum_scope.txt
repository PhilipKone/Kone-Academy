4.2.2 Curriculum Scope and Sequence

Level 1 of the Kone Academy platform targets learners ages 7-11, corresponding to Ghanaian upper primary and junior secondary education. The curriculum scope and sequence reflect developmental appropriateness, Ghanaian curriculum standards, and research on effective computational thinking progression for this age group. This section details the content organization, pedagogical approach, and assessment integration that characterize Level 1 implementation.

**Age-Appropriate Activities (Ages 7-11)**

The curriculum recognizes substantial developmental variation within the 7-11 age range, organizing content into three bands that accommodate different readiness levels while maintaining coherent progression. The early band (ages 7-8) emphasizes concrete, exploratory activities where learners manipulate pre-built programs and observe outcomes rather than constructing complex algorithms from scratch. Activities include sequencing story events, directing character movements through mazes, and simple pattern completion exercises that introduce computational concepts through play rather than explicit instruction.

The middle band (ages 9-10) introduces systematic algorithm construction, with learners creating programs that solve defined problems through sequences, loops, and conditionals. Projects at this level include interactive stories with branching narratives, simple games with scoring and win conditions, and automation tools that perform repetitive tasks. The pedagogical approach combines guided instruction with creative exploration, providing scaffolding for core concepts while enabling learner choice in project directions.

The advanced band (ages 11+) prepares learners for Level 2 transition through projects that approximate real-world programming complexity. Learners create multi-step applications, debug intentionally flawed programs, and engage in peer code review. This band emphasizes the habits of mind that characterize effective programmers—systematic debugging, incremental development, and documentation—while maintaining the playful engagement that sustains motivation.

**Computational Thinking Concepts**

The Level 1 curriculum systematically develops five core computational thinking concepts aligned with Wing's (2006) framework and Ghanaian curriculum standards: decomposition, pattern recognition, abstraction, algorithm design, and debugging.

Decomposition is introduced through activities that break complex problems into manageable steps. Learners plan multi-step projects—creating animations, designing games, building interactive stories—by identifying discrete components and sequencing their implementation. Ghanaian cultural contexts provide meaningful decomposition opportunities: planning a traditional ceremony involves breaking the event into preparation, execution, and cleanup phases, each with sub-components that map naturally to computational decomposition.

Pattern recognition develops through activities where learners identify recurring sequences and create generalizable solutions. Music rhythm exercises, textile pattern design, and agricultural cycle modeling provide culturally-familiar contexts for identifying patterns. Learners generalize specific instances into rules that can generate multiple outputs, developing the rule-based thinking that underlies both programming and mathematical reasoning.

Abstraction is scaffolded through the transition from concrete to generalized representations. Early activities work with specific instances—particular characters, specific locations, defined quantities. As learners progress, they create variables and functions that enable single programs to handle multiple scenarios. This progression mirrors mathematical abstraction development while maintaining concrete anchors that prevent the cognitive overload that premature abstraction can create.

Algorithm design—the heart of programming—is developed through progressive complexity. Learners begin with linear sequences, add conditional branching for decision-making, incorporate loops for repetition, and eventually combine these elements into sophisticated multi-step algorithms. Each algorithmic structure is introduced through worked examples, practiced with scaffolding, and applied in creative projects that demonstrate understanding beyond mere reproduction.

Debugging is treated not as error correction but as systematic problem-solving methodology. The curriculum explicitly teaches debugging strategies: isolating problematic code segments, testing incremental changes, and comparing expected versus actual behavior. Learners engage with intentionally flawed programs, practicing debugging in controlled contexts before encountering errors in their own work. This approach inverts the traditional pattern where debugging is learned reactively through frustration, instead developing debugging as a deliberate skill.

**Project-Based Learning**

Level 1 employs project-based learning as its primary pedagogical mode, with learners spending approximately 70% of their time creating and refining projects rather than completing exercises or receiving direct instruction. This emphasis on creation aligns with constructionist principles and research documenting that programming concepts learned through project development produce deeper understanding than concepts presented through demonstration or tutorial.

Projects are organized into thematic categories that provide coherence while accommodating diverse interests. The "Digital Stories" category includes narrative projects—interactive fiction, animated tales, informational presentations—that develop sequencing and event handling concepts. "Game Design" projects introduce game mechanics, user input processing, and scoring systems while building engagement through creation of playable experiences. "Useful Tools" projects address practical applications—calculators, converters, planners—that demonstrate computing utility for everyday problems. "Art and Music" projects explore creative computing, developing understanding of randomness, repetition, and procedural generation through visual and audio outputs.

Each project category includes progressive difficulty levels, ensuring that learners can find appropriately challenging projects regardless of prior experience. Project templates provide starting points for learners needing structure, while open-ended challenges enable advanced learners to extend projects beyond baseline requirements. This differentiation within project-based learning accommodates heterogeneous classrooms where learners enter with varying preparation and progress at different rates.

**Assessment Integration**

Assessment in Level 1 is embedded throughout learning activities rather than segregated into distinct testing events. This approach aligns with research on authentic assessment and accommodates infrastructure constraints that might make formal testing impractical.

Diagnostic assessments at module beginnings identify prior knowledge and readiness, enabling the platform to adjust scaffolding levels and suggest appropriate starting points. These lightweight assessments employ interactive activities—completing partial programs, predicting program outputs, identifying algorithmic errors—that assess understanding through performance rather than self-report or multiple-choice selection.

Formative assessment occurs continuously through learning analytics that capture process data: sequencing of block placement, debugging attempts, time-on-task, and help resource utilization. These data inform adaptive scaffolding adjustments and provide teachers with visibility into learner engagement and struggle patterns. Rather than merely recording correct/incorrect outcomes, process-focused assessment captures how learners approach problems, revealing misconceptions and learning strategies invisible to endpoint evaluation.

Summative assessment at level completion employs portfolio evaluation, with learners compiling selected projects, reflective commentaries, and debugging documentation that demonstrate computational thinking development. Portfolio rubrics assess not merely technical correctness but problem-solving sophistication, creative expression, and growth over time. This portfolio approach accommodates varying pacing and acknowledges diverse forms of excellence, while generating evidence suitable for credentialing or progression to Level 2.

Self-assessment is scaffolded through reflection prompts that encourage learners to evaluate their own work, identify areas for improvement, and set goals for future projects. These metacognitive practices develop self-directed learning capabilities that extend beyond Kone Academy, positioning learners as active managers of their own educational trajectories.

**Conclusion**

Level 1's curriculum scope and sequence translate research on developmental appropriateness, computational thinking progression, and effective pedagogy into concrete learning experiences for Ghanaian primary learners. By organizing content into age-bands, systematically developing core concepts, emphasizing project-based learning, and integrating authentic assessment, the platform provides a foundation for computational thinking development that prepares learners for Level 2 while remaining engaging and accessible for the full 7-11 age range.
